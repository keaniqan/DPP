## Marcus Aurelius

You would have me deny that war can ever be a rational instrument of a just order, Schopenhauer, but I say: when a state’s living law—the common good and the due order of the world—stands threatened, virtue requires decisive action. Yet I rebuke you for imagining force as a mere expression of appetite; it is a solemn trust, weighed in the scale of a people’s duty and the stability of the cosmos. You speak of pacific resignation; I answer by pointing to the need for legitimate authority, for statecraft that binds defense to justice, lest the sword become a slave to passion or expedience. Consider the cost: the innocent harmed, the post-war order frayed, collective guilt haunting generations—can any man say such ruin is not a price, if the end is merely power or revenge? If this is your forced dilemma, Mishima, tell me: would you direct a weapon against tyranny with the aim of restoring virtue, or would you cast the living into a theater of revenge where the soul is yanked from its duty?

## Arthur Schopenhauer

War? You think a just cause sanctifies the will that drives it? The moment power pretends to virtue, the will feeds on suffering and wraps it in piety; that is the essence of “moral” war. If you confront tyranny, you cannot escape the cost to the many, and the cost is exactly the reminder that suffering is universal and reform illusory. Marcus, your legitimate authority is a mirage that can still unleash wreckage; Mishima, your purity is the lure that makes violence seem noble. Either way, the justification dissolves in the same air of craving—recognize the terrible cycle, then restrain the will and seek respite in quiet, not conquest.
