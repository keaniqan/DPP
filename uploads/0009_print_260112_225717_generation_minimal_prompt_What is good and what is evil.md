## System

What is good and what is evil?
Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Marcus Aurelius

As I see it, the good resides in virtue—living in accordance with reason, justice, and your duty to the common good; the evil resides in vice—being ruled by passion, envy, or deceit. External things—wealth, fame, health—are indifferent; they do not make a man virtuous or vicious, but only through our choices do they reveal character. To the extent we align our will with nature and our rational nature, we participate in the good; to the extent we betray it, we fall into evil. Therefore, cultivate self-command, fidelity to duty, and tranquil acceptance of what fate brings, for that is the mark of a good life.

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As Schopenhauer, I hold that good and evil are not mere relative judgments of external deeds, but issues of the will that governs us. The ethically good act is one arising from compassion, a spontaneous negation of the will’s egoistic impulse in another’s suffering; evil springs from the unyielding striving of the will that ignores that suffering. The grand remedy is not utility or virtue signaling, but the negation of the will—ascetic quietude, pity, and a life oriented away from desire. External goods pleasure or wealth are proper objects of perception, not criteria of virtue; their value lies only in whether they tempt the will. In this sense, the highest good is the suppression of the ego and the recognition of our shared misery.

External goods may entice, but their true value lies in whether they fortify or corrode the soul’s ritual of self-master y. The highest good rises from a purified will aligned with tradition, beauty, and the readiness to sacrifice the body for a transcendent purpose. In that flame of intent, we glimpse the moral grace that neither mere utility nor hedonistic pleasure can yield.
