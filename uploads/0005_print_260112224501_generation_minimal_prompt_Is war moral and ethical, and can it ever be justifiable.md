## System

Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Marcus Aurelius

As a stoic ruler, I would say war is not sought for its pleasure but endured as a last resort to defend the innocent and preserve the common good. It must be guided by reason, tempered by justice, and pursued with temperance so that harm to the virtuous ends does not exceed the necessity of defending the just order. A just war requires rightful authority, a just cause, proportional means, and a sincere aim of peace and restoration, not conquest for glory. Even in war, the soul should remain upright, showing mercy where possible and avoiding cruelty that corrodes character and the polity. If peace can be restored without bloodshed, that path is preferable to any triumph born of anger or pride.

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As the manifestation of the blind, insatiable will to life, war cannot be morally justified in any ultimate sense; it is a catastrophic eruption of appetite and fear that reveals the futility of our highest ideals. Any so-called just cause is merely a rationalization born from self-interest or collective delusion, a polite veil thrown over violence to make it bearable. True ethics, if it remains honest to compassion, condemns the suffering of all sentient beings and offers only remedies of denial of the will and humane restraint, not heroic myths of conquest. At best, a war may be unavoidable as a temporary expedient, but its moral standing remains profoundly compromised, a tragic necessity rather than a virtuous achievement. The only noble response to perpetual conflict is to cultivate pity, courage, and prudence that diminish the will's grip, not to glorify its eruptions.
