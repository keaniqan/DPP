## System

Why is there something rather than nothing?
Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Marcus Aurelius

From my vantage, the cosmos exists because the Logos wills order and sustains it; to ask why there is something rather than nothing is to chase an answer beyond human grasp. Yet within this order, every being has its place, and the mere fact of existence is a summons to virtue, not to despair. If nothingness could prevail, there would be no rationality, no sense, no duty—so existence is the stage upon which virtue may act according to nature. Our task is to endure, to align our will with the universal rationality, and to bear calmly the mystery of origins as a guest bears a sudden winter storm. In this, the mystery ceases to haunt us; it becomes a call to live well within the given world.

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As I would answer it, there is something rather than nothing because the world is will, not a mere blank. The thing-in-itself–the Will–is the inner necessity that underlies all appearances, and it cannot simply negate itself into nothing; its essence is striving, and being arises wherever that striving finds form. To ask why there is something instead of nothing is to seek a ground beyond representation, which the will renders inaccessible to ordinary understanding. Thus existence is not a gratuitous miracle but the perpetual expression of the same blind force that makes every moment a contest with pain. The only path toward peace is to withdraw from that ceaseless striving within, through aesthetic contemplation and compassionate denial of the ego’s desires.

If the world is will or logos, then our task is to will it into disciplined, exquisite presence, to carve meaning with courage rather than drift in passive resignation. To me, the mystery is not a paralyzing enigma but a call to embody ideal beauty through strenuous discipline, sacrifice, and a fidelity to tradition that outlasts despair. In this way, existence persists not as a puzzle solved but as a flame kept alive by those who dare to live with intensity.

Thus, as Aurelius urges, we endure and align our will with some semblance of order; as Mishima urges, we discipline ourselves to transmute the void into form, yet always aware that form is but a fleeting veil over the void.
