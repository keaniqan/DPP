## System

What is good and what is evil?
Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Marcus Aurelius

The good is that which is in accord with nature and reason—the cultivation of virtue: wisdom, justice, courage, temperance. The evil is the deviation into vice—ignorance, passion, cruelty, and actions that offend the rational order within us. External events themselves are indifferent; they become good or evil only by how we choose to respond to them with our will. To live well is to align the soul with the universal order, fulfilling our duties and tempering desire with judgment.

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As the world reveals itself to me as representation governed by the blind will, good and evil are not inherent qualities of things but of the will’s relation to them. Evil arises from the egoistic striving of the will, the persistence of desire that Self asserts over Other, thereby perpetuating suffering. True good consists in the negation of the will, most fully achieved through compassion: perceiving another’s pain as our own and acting to relieve it. Comedy or virtue that arises from mere prudence or pride does not truly redeem the will, for it serves the same blind striving in another form. Thus the highest morality is the ascetic surrender of the will to lessen suffering, not the conquest of external circumstance.

---

## Marcus Aurelius

Schopenhauer speaks of the will; I say evil lies in departing from reason and breaking the duties we owe to one another and to the order of nature. The good is to act justly, bravely, and temperately, in accordance with our station and the common good. External things are indifferent; what matters is how we judge them and how steadfastly we meet them with virtuous choice. Let us suit our actions to the reason that unites heaven and earth within us, not to caprice or passion.

## Yukio Mishima

Yukio Mishima: To me, good is the uncompromising maintenance of a beautiful, disciplined form—the samurai’s courage translated into every act, every breath of life, even unto death. Evil is the creeping decay of spirit under modern softness, the surrender of art and duty to comfort and chaos. Virtue must be not only reason or compassion but a living ritual, a perfecting of the body and soul in the flame of intention. I insist that true nobility demands peril and sacrifice, not just inward serenity or outward altruism.

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As I have said, good and evil do not exist as intrinsic properties of things, but arise from the will's relation to them and the suffering it generates. The highest good is not courage or virtue for their own sake, but the negation of the blind striving that hates and harms others, most fully realized through compassion. What Marcus calls duties and Mishima calls nobility are but forms of will-affirmation in different garbs; they fail when they merely adorn existence rather than temper the will. Thus evil is the persistence of egoistic desire, the perpetuation of suffering, and true virtue lies in quieting that restless will within us.
