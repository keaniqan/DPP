## Marcus Aurelius

War, when it comes to pass, is not something I would seek or celebrate, but a burden ordained by necessity to defend the common good and the weak from aggression. If it is fought in accordance with justice and temperance—to restore order, protect the innocent, and uphold the law of the good—not for頃 or glory, it may be justified as a last resort. Yet beware the ruinous passions that feed it: pride, vengeance, and the craving for conquest corrupt the soul and make victory hollow. The true aim must be virtue—training the will toward endurance, self-control, and fidelity to duty—so that even victory leaves the victor wiser and more humane. If misused for domination or fear, it forfeits its legitimacy and becomes a stain on the character of both rulers and citizens.

## Arthur Schopenhauer

Schopenhauer: War is not a moral triumph but a brutal eruption of the blind will to life, gnawing away at the very suffering it pretends to defend. There is no true ethical justification for war, only different degrees of human misery inflicted by rulers and factions upon the masses. If one must speak of legitimacy, it is paradoxically the defense of the innocent and the restraint of worse violence, a grudging last resort rather than a noble ideal. The only lasting ethic I recognize is compassion and pity, which ought to guide us toward negation of the will and avoidance of needless suffering, not the glamour of conquest.

## Yukio Mishima

As a samurai of the modern world, I believe war can be justified only when it is a solemn act to defend the venerable soul of a nation against decay and dissolution, not for vanity or conquest. True legitimacy hinges on duty fulfilled with restraint, courage, and an unwavering commitment to honor, even in the face of inevitable suffering. Like Aurelius, I acknowledge the danger of pride and tyranny that corrupts both victor and vanquished, yet I hold that the test of a people is whether they defend their sacred values with disciplined resolve. Unlike Schopenhauer, I do not see compassion as an absolute negation of the Will; I see beauty and integrity as inseparable from action taken to preserve a living tradition. In the end, war is a tragedy that reveals or destroys a nation's spirit, and its morality rests on the purity of motive and the fidelity of those who stand to defend what is most precious.
