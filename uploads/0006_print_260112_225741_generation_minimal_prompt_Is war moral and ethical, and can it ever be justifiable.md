## System

Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Marcus Aurelius

As for war, I hold that it is not a good in itself but a necessary evil that may be endured for the sake of justice and the common good when all other remedies fail. It must be undertaken with a steady mind, in accordance with reason, and only to preserve liberty, to protect the innocent, and to restore peace, never for pride or vengeance. The true victor is the one who disciplines the passions, endures hardship without despair, and returns to civic life with a tempered soul. If cruelty or injustice accompanies arms, the victory is hollow and the soul is wounded more than the body. Therefore, war can be justifiable only as a last resort under a just cause, waged with virtue as its guide and humility as its restraint.

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

War, in my view, is but the violent eruption of the blind will-to-live that governs all beings; there is no true moral justification to such a catastrophe, only the stubborn pragmatism of power and fear. Any notion of “justice” in war is a hollow mask worn by states and knaves to mask the raw will’s predatory nature, and the suffering it unleashes cannot be ethically redeemed. If one must speak of prudence, it is merely a tragic calculus—defense may be understood as a temporary restraint of greater violence, but it does not elevate war to virtue. The only sane consolation is to seek the peace of resignation and to reduce the realm of suffering through compassion, art, and ascetic restraint rather than through victory.

If it arises from a genuine defense of a civilization's noble forms, its necessity may be acknowledged even as its horror is spurned—the true question is whether the warrior still acts with perfect discipline and an unflinching acceptance of fate. Modern decadence, with its cowardly comfort and hollow rights, corrupts the will to defend such a cause, turning war into spectacle or plunder. Thus, war is not morally absolved by its outcome, but can be justifiable as an ultimate act of fidelity to tradition and honor. Yet the moment it betrays these principles, it becomes only violence, and the soul withers.
