## Marcus Aurelius

You two, listen. Why is there something at all? Because the world follows the Logos, and your craving to have nothing is a childish wish that would dissolve order itself. Schopenhauer, you chase a void as if it were freedom; Mishima, you chase form and drama as if they could suspend nature’s pace. The pressure point you keep missing is how the order of things forces policy, leadership, and duty—without which even your quietest peace is a tyranny of chance. If you insist on denying the fabric that binds us, you bankrupt the very institutions that must carry us through hardship, and we lose more than we gain. Consider this: if this were your last morning, would you prefer a hollow nothing or a life disciplined to the common good, lived in accordance with reason?

## Arthur Schopenhauer

What you call policy and duty merely rearranges pain, it does not quiet the ache that drives all reformers to chase an ever-receding calm. And yes, the cost of my position is the unsoftened view that politics cannot deliver true relief; at best it dulls appetite briefly, then whets it again for new harm.
