## Marcus Aurelius

Schopenhauer, your endless will is the root of all suffering, and yet you mistake appetite for the measure of what is good. I say good lies in virtue aligned with reason and service to the common governance of nature, not in the self-indulgent denial or dramatic self-flagellation you prize. Mishima, your code of honor and ritual ends in spectacle; I insist true duty is steady daily virtue under judgment of reason, not the theater of heroism that calamity loves. Consider this: if we fail to restrain the will in institutions that shape many lives, we pile collective ruin on future generations. If you must choose, endure the ordinary burden rightly and you will endure the cosmos; if you refuse, you invite chaos dressed as meaning.

## Arthur Schopenhauer

If you insist on public virtue as cure, you double the harm by binding others to a fiction that only rituals can restrain, and you leave the real pain intact. The cost I acknowledge is that renunciation may seem brittle, but it is the only plausible pause in the chase; without it, the cycle of striving consumes generations. Tell me, will your system endure the contempt of future ages when the will resumes its hungry march?
