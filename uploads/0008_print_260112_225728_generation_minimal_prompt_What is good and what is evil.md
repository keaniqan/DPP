## System

Why is there something rather than nothing?
Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

As I have long argued, there is something rather than nothing because the world is will and representation; the thing-in-itself is the blind, insatiable will whose very essence is to persevere in striving. Nothingness would negate the very ground of sensation and thought, hence cannot be; where there is representation, there is a manifesting force that cannot simply cease. The so-called “why” dissolves into a more fundamental inquiry: why does there exist at all a ground, not a cessation, of striving? There is no grand purpose or creator trialed by reason, only the perpetual, arduous compulsion of the will that underlies all appearances. In this light, existence is the brutish, undeniable theatre of suffering, yet it is the only possible form the will can take.

---

## Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius: In the vast theatre of the cosmos, I see no ground for nothingness, for the Reason that orders all things would still be present as the source of unity and coherence. If there were truly nothing, there would be neither mind to behold nor order to apprehend, which is contrary to the very nature of the universe. Therefore existence is the natural expression of a rational plan, albeit one often hidden from our petty desires. Our task is to align our will with Nature and to bear with virtue whatever arises, for in that harmony lies the only true peace.

## Yukio Mishima

Yukio Mishima: I would say that nothingness is the dream of modern despair, while existence insists on the body’s sovereign presence—its form, its breath, its ritual. If there were no something, there would be no arena for courage, no stage for art, no possibility of self-transcendence through perfected form. Therefore, existence persists not as a rational scheme but as an aesthetic imperative: to mold life into a perfect, uncluttered expression before the inexorable fade. In this light, the question becomes how one can live so intensely as to make the nothingness retreat before the glare of a fully realized self.

Therefore there is something not by whim, but by the steadfast requirement of Nature’s plan, and our task is to align our will with that plan through virtue. Obstacles and pain prove the arena where a mind trained in discipline can endure and endure rightly.

Nothingness cannot "be" for that which is, since to negate representation would annihilate the very thing that could appear to us. Thus there is something rather than nothing because the will, as thing-in-itself, must endure and strive; there is no ultimate void capable of stopping the incessant thrust of being. To ask why this ground exists is to ask for a purpose where there is only motive force and endless striving, veiled beneath the veil of appearances. In this light, existence is not a plan but the stubborn necessity of the will’s persistence, and the question itself dissolves into a further question about the nature of that ground.
