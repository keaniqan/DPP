## System

Participants: Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Schopenhauer, Yukio Mishima

---

## Arthur Schopenhauer

The question pierces the veil of appearances to a ground we shall not easily reach; Kant alone grants us the distinction, but not the ultimate reason why anything should be rather than nothing. The world, as I teach, is the representation of the blind will-to-live; existence is the perpetual striving of this, not a derivable ground but the very necessity of its own being manifested. Therefore there is something because the will must will, and through willing produce phenomena—yet this is not an intelligible justification but a grim fact of our experience, laden with pain. The only sane response is not to seek triumphantly for a why, but to recognize the will as our universal source and seek liberation through negation of the will.

## Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius: The question presses upon us as men who inhabit this rational order; if there were nothing, nothing would be fit to be governed by reason or to be a field for virtue. The cosmos is an ordered whole, bound by the Logos, and our task is to align our will with that order, not pry secrets beyond our nature. If existence arises from the needs of the whole, then our duty is to live justly, endure what happens with equanimity, and use our brief time to cultivate virtue. Whether the ground of being can be fully named or not, we must act as rational beings within it. So let us accept the mystery with gratitude and discipline our souls to live according to nature.
